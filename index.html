Critical Control
vs Quality Control

Know the difference between keeping food safe and making food great.

🛡️
CCP — Critical Control Point A step where a mistake can make someone sick. These are non-negotiable — food safety comes first, always.
⭐
QC — Quality Control A check to make your food look, taste, and feel excellent. Getting it wrong means poor results — not illness.
🍓 Fruit Salad
Critical Control Points (Safety)
Quality Control (Excellence)
🧼
Handwashing
What
Wash hands with soap and warm water for 20 seconds before touching any ready-to-eat fruit.
Why
Prevents bacteria and viruses from your hands transferring to food that won't be cooked.
Risk
Harmful pathogens like E. coli could contaminate the fruit → food poisoning.
🔪
Consistent Cut Size
What
Use the claw and bridge grip to cut fruit into even, bite-sized pieces (~2 cm).
Why
Consistent sizing creates a pleasant texture and an appealing, professional presentation.
Risk
Uneven chunks make the salad hard to eat and visually unappealing.
🟩
Green Board Only
What
Use a dedicated green chopping board and clean knife exclusively for fruit and vegetables.
Why
Prevents cross-contamination from surfaces previously used for other high-risk foods.
Risk
Bacteria like Salmonella could transfer to ready-to-eat fruit → severe illness.
🍋
Prevent Browning
What
Squeeze fresh lemon or citrus juice over cut apple and banana immediately after cutting.
Why
Acid stops the flesh from oxidising (enzymic browning) when exposed to air.
Risk
Apple and banana turn brown within minutes → looks bruised and unappetising.
🚿
Wash All Produce
What
Wash all fresh fruits under clean running water before peeling or cutting.
Why
Removes physical hazards (dirt, pesticides) and reduces bacteria on the skin.
Risk
The knife blade drags bacteria or dirt from unwashed skin directly into the edible flesh.
🎨
Colour Variety
What
Deliberately include a mix of differently coloured fruits — red, green, yellow, orange.
Why
Meets the Aesthetics (A) criterion in your ACCESSFM specification. Food should look vibrant.
Risk
A single-colour salad looks monotonous and fails your design spec.
❄️
Chill Below 5°C
What
Store finished fruit salad in the fridge at or below 5°C if not eaten immediately.
Why
Temperature control slows or stops harmful bacterial growth — it's a critical food safety limit.
Risk
Bacteria multiply rapidly at room temperature in a moist dish → unsafe to eat.
⚖️
Balanced Proportions
What
Use a similar volume of each fruit so no single ingredient dominates the bowl.
Why
Ensures every spoonful has variety and meets your ACCESSFM Function statement.
Risk
Too much of one fruit makes it feel undesigned — just a pile, not a crafted dish.
🍝 Pasta Salad
Critical Control Points (Safety)
Quality Control (Excellence)
🧼
Handwashing First
What
Wash hands with soap for 20 seconds before handling any ingredient.
Why
Removes bacteria before you touch food. Non-negotiable at the start of every practical.
Risk
Bacteria from hands contaminate vegetables or pasta → food poisoning.
⏱️
Cook Pasta Al Dente
What
Taste pasta one minute before the packet time — it should be firm to the bite.
Why
Pasta continues cooking after draining. Al dente texture holds up when tossed with dressing.
Risk
Overcooked pasta goes mushy and falls apart into a starchy, unpleasant paste.
🔥
Pan Handle Safety
What
Always turn pan handles inward on the hob. Use oven gloves when handling hot pots.
Why
Prevents the pan from being knocked off the hob, causing severe burns and scalds.
Risk
A knocked pan of boiling water → serious scalding injury. This is a physical hazard CCP.
🚰
Drain Thoroughly
What
Drain pasta in a colander and shake to remove all excess cooking water.
Why
Water in the pasta dilutes dressing and stops it coating the pasta properly.
Risk
Watery salad where dressing slides off → bland, poorly seasoned dish.
🧊
Cool Before Dressing
What
Cool cooked pasta rapidly (rinse under cold water) before adding mayonnaise or perishable dressings.
Why
Warm pasta + mayonnaise creates ideal conditions for rapid bacterial growth.
Risk
Bacteria multiply in the warm, moist environment → food unsafe to eat in minutes.
🥦
Uniform Vegetable Dice
What
Dice vegetables into small, uniform pieces using the claw grip.
Why
Every forkful should have a balanced mix of pasta, veg, and dressing.
Risk
Oversized vegetable chunks overpower the pasta and create uneven eating experience.
⚠️
Allergen Control
What
Keep allergens (gluten, dairy, eggs in dressing) strictly separate. Use clean utensils.
Why
Allergens are a chemical food safety hazard. The safe limit is zero contact.
Risk
Even a trace of an allergen can trigger a severe or life-threatening allergic reaction.
🧂
Season and Taste
What
Taste the finished salad and adjust seasoning before serving.
Why
Pasta absorbs salt from cooking water. Seasoning needs adjusting after the dressing is added.
Risk
Bland, unseasoned dish that fails your taste/function design criteria.
🥚 Egg Dish
Critical Control Points (Safety)
Quality Control (Excellence)
🌡️
Cook Eggs Fully
What
Cook eggs until both yolk and white are fully set with no runny parts.
Why
Heat destroys harmful bacteria that are naturally present in raw eggs.
Risk
Undercooked eggs can harbour Salmonella → severe food poisoning, especially risky for young people.
⏱️
Precise Timing
What
Use a timer — never guess boiling or cooking time for eggs.
Why
Egg proteins set at precise temperatures. Even 2 extra minutes changes the texture completely.
Risk
Overcooking creates a grey-green sulphur ring around the yolk and rubbery whites.
🧼
Wash After Raw Egg
What
Wash hands, boards, and utensils with hot soapy water immediately after touching raw eggs or shells.
Why
Raw eggshells carry bacteria that easily transfer to ready-to-eat surfaces.
Risk
Bacteria cross-contaminates other ingredients → spreads illness to anyone who eats the dish.
🧊
Ice Bath After Boiling
What
Plunge boiled eggs into ice-cold water immediately after cooking.
Why
Stops internal cooking instantly. Also shrinks the egg away from the shell → much easier peeling.
Risk
Eggs continue cooking → shell sticks and tears the white, leaving a messy, uneven result.
🐚
Check for Shell
What
Carefully inspect peeled eggs and finished dish for any shell fragments before serving.
Why
Eggshell is a hard physical hazard that can cut the mouth or cause a choking hazard.
Risk
A sharp shell fragment injures the consumer's mouth → physical hazard incident.
🥄
Gentle Mixing
What
Fold mayonnaise or yoghurt into chopped egg until just combined — don't over-mix.
Why
Gentle folding keeps a slightly chunky texture that has a far better mouthfeel.
Risk
Over-mixing turns egg into a homogenous, unappetising paste — no texture at all.
❄️
Refrigerate Quickly
What
Store finished egg dish in the fridge at or below 5°C immediately after making.
Why
Eggs are high-protein, high-moisture — a perfect breeding ground for bacteria at room temperature.
Risk
Leaving egg dish out allows explosive bacterial growth in under two hours.
🎨
Garnish and Present
What
Add a garnish (paprika, chives, cress) and present on a clean white dish or plate.
Why
Meets the Aesthetics (A) criterion in ACCESSFM. We eat with our eyes first.
Risk
A plain, ungarnished dish looks unfinished and scores poorly on presentation.

⚡ Quick Reference — CCP vs QC

Feature 🛡️ Critical Control Point (CCP) ⭐ Quality Control (QC)
Purpose Prevent food safety hazards Ensure great taste, texture & look
If missed… Someone could get seriously ill Food looks or tastes bad
Examples Handwashing, cooking temperature, cross-contamination prevention Consistent cut size, preventing browning, timing pasta
Hazard type Biological, chemical, physical Aesthetic & sensory
On your planning sheet Write exactly WHY the control prevents illness Write exactly HOW it improves the dish
First step — always Step 1 is ALWAYS handwashing with a full CCP explanation — every single practical.
